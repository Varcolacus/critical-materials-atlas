Who does the EU really depend on?

Correcting EU import statistics for the transit-port distortion — a method note.

The headline question — "which countries is the EU import-dependent on for a critical product?" — has an obvious answer in the public data that is wrong, and a correct answer that takes domain knowledge to extract. This note documents the difference.

The correction in one identity

For extra-EU imports, the Eurostat Comext partner field is the country of origin. That single fact separates two views of the same data:

  • Naive — by importing member state. Ranks dependence by which EU country clears the goods. Distorted by the Rotterdam/Antwerp effect: imports are customs-cleared in NL/BE ports (or refined in hubs such as Umicore in Belgium) but bound elsewhere. This measures logistics geography, not dependence. It is the trap.
  • Corrected — EU as one entity, by country of origin. Aggregates the 27 member states into a single importer and ranks by true origin. This is the answer.

Across all thirty-two the naive and corrected panels disagree on the single most important fact — who the EU depends on — and only the corrected one is true. China is the real origin for ten, but far from a majority — and the two sharpest dependencies are not China at all: beryllium is 100% United States and boron 98% Turkey. The rest span South Africa, Brazil, Japan, Vietnam, Guinea, Gabon, Russia, Chile, Algeria/Qatar, Norway, Mexico, Kazakhstan and Tajikistan — almost none visible in the member-state view. The method surfaces whatever the transit data hides; it is not a China-finding machine.

A second correction: refined origin vs. the mine

Even the corrected origin is often just the refinery, not the source. Under customs rules refining counts as "origin": when China imports cobalt ore, refines it and re-exports, the metal becomes Chinese-origin and the trade record stops there. One layer deeper, the picture changes again:

So the EU's apparent China dependence on cobalt is, upstream, a Congo mining dependence funnelled through a Chinese refinery — China is the chokepoint, not the source. Trade data alone cannot show this; it needs an overlay of USGS mine-production statistics. No existing trade-flow tool (OEC, Resource Trade Earth, Comtrade dashboards) does it — they stop at the refiner. The interactive tool shows both layers together and draws the implied mine → refiner → EU chain.

Why it can't be faked from the raw download

Bloc aggregates double-count. The extract carries bloc reporters and partners (EU, EA, EA21, EU27_2020, EXT_*, WORLD) alongside the 27 member states. Summing without dropping them inflates origin totals ~4.6× — yet leaves the shares unchanged, so the error is invisible to anyone who validates on percentages alone.
The Rotterdam/Antwerp transit effect. Member-state import figures attribute goods to the point of customs clearance, not consumption. Without the origin correction the largest "dependency" is a port.
Confidential & suppressed flows. For low-volume strategic materials, sensitive trade lines are withheld or masked in Comext; they must be detected and handled, not silently summed as zero.
CN8 code vintages. Eight-digit codes split and are reclassified over time (rare-earth magnets 8505 11 10 exists only from 2023; gallium and germanium run 2010–2024). Series breaks must be respected, not stitched blindly.
Origin ≠ consignment, and units. The partner-as-origin identity holds only for extra-EU flows; quantities arrive as QUANTITY_IN_100KG and need conversion. Small choices that quietly corrupt a ranking if missed.

Why the time series matters

Because gallium and germanium carry 15 years of data, the dependency is not a snapshot but a trajectory. Gallium's China-origin share runs 96.8% (2022) → 85% (2023) → 68% (2024) as Canada and Russia step in — China's July-2023 export controls visible directly in the customs record. Dependency that moves under geopolitical pressure is the analytically interesting object, and it only appears once the origin correction is applied.

Data & reproducibility

EU import origin: Eurostat Comext, dataset DS-045409, extra-EU imports, annual. The pipeline is base R (no package installs), parameterised by CN code, and refreshes from the live API — so any product, and any later year, is one command away.

Global bilateral trade (the interactive map / globe / flow): primary source UN Comtrade, used in its reconciled, mirror-harmonised form CEPII BACI (Gaulier, G. & Zignago, S., 2010, BACI: International Trade Database at the Product-Level, CEPII WP 2010-23) — release HS22 V202501, year 2023. Mine production: USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries (approximate). Refining shares: IEA Critical Minerals Outlook (approximate). All sources are public; figures are rounded.
