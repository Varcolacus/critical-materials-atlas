Method · robustness

Do the findings survive a harder test?

The Trends page says concentration is rising for a set of materials. That rests on a Mann-Kendall test which assumes each year is independent — but concentration series are serially correlated, which overstates significance. Here the same claims are re-run under an autocorrelation-robust test and split across the 2017 data-vintage join. What survives, we can stand behind; what doesn’t, we say so.

Two adversarial checks on the rising-concentration claim. (1) Hamed-Rao (1998) — the standard fix for trend tests on autocorrelated data: it inflates the Mann-Kendall variance by the significant rank-autocorrelations, so a serially-correlated series needs a stronger signal to count as significant. (2) Splice sub-period — each trend re-estimated separately on the HS02 window (2002–2016) and the HS17 window (2017–2024); a trend that keeps its sign in both halves is not an artifact of the 2017 vintage join. Both significance screens use Benjamini-Hochberg FDR across all 32 materials. Computed by build_robustness.py.

The rising-concentration materials, under the harder tests

Materials flagged with a significant rising export-HHI by the standard test, then re-checked. “Var ×” is the Hamed-Rao variance inflation from positive autocorrelation (≥1; this is a deliberately conservative one-sided correction — serial correlation may only make the test harder, never easier).
