Method · economic complexity

What only a few countries can export

Revealed comparative advantage (Balassa) asks who is a genuinely competitive exporter of each material. Ubiquity — how few countries clear that bar — is a market-revealed strategic signal, and relatedness shows which materials the same countries tend to command together.

What this adds. Using the one complete country×material matrix we have — exports — we compute Balassa RCA (a country is a competitive exporter of a material when its export share of that material exceeds its overall export share), a robust capability score, and relatedness (do the same countries export both?). Ubiquity = how many countries clear RCA≥1: a low number means only a handful of countries can competitively supply it — a market signal of relative scarcity beside the mine-concentration story. Caveat: RCA here is computed over the 32-material matrix only, so it is specialization within critical materials, not economy-wide Balassa RCA — small and re-export economies can surface as "competitive", and the RCA≥1 threshold makes ubiquity sensitive to thin flows and HS6 pooling. Mine/refine data are top-N (too sparse for full RCA); mine leader shown for contrast. Computed by build_complexity.py from public data.

The most capable exporters

Capability = the sum of 1/ubiquity over the materials a country competitively exports (RCA≥1) — it rewards commanding many materials that few others can. Leaders mix large industrial economies with smaller open / re-export economies — read this as export specialization, not industrial capability per se.
