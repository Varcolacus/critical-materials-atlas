Method · network position

The trade routes concentration misses

Top-exporter share tells you how concentrated a material's trade is. It says nothing about who sits on the routes. Treating each material's bilateral flows as a directed network surfaces the brokers and processing hubs that the customs ledger hides — and a node-removal test shows how far the network fragments without them.

What this adds. A country can hold a small export share yet be the node everything routes through. We build each material's directed, value-weighted trade network and rank countries by betweenness (brokerage) and PageRank (recursive flow importance), then classify the top broker by corroborating it against the refining stage: a processing chokepoint is also the top refiner (network centrality coincides with refining dominance), a redistribution hub is a big importer that re-distributes, a transit hub is a curated entrepot (re-export, not control). The last column is a resilience test: the share of supply→demand routes that break when that single node is removed. Read with care: betweenness on a trade network also reflects import size — a big buyer scores high without "controlling" anything — so treat this as trade-routing centrality (exploratory, hypothesis-generating), and "processing chokepoint" as "most-central and the top refiner", not proof of control.
