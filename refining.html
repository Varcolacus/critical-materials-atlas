The refining wedge

Every metal is traded twice — once as ore or concentrate, and again as refined or first-processed metal. When the refined stage is more geographically concentrated than the ore, control has moved from the mine to the furnace: the chokepoint is who processes, not who digs. We measure both stages from the same bilateral-trade source and rank where that wedge is widest.

The method, and its honest scope

For each metal we take its ore/concentrate HS-2017 line and its refined / first-processed basket (refined metal plus the dominant ferroalloy or intermediate route, so the “refined” stage reflects the form actually traded — e.g. ferronickel for nickel, ferrochrome for chromium). For each stage we compute the exporter Herfindahl (HHI) on trade value, then the wedge = HHI(refined) − HHI(ore). Positive = processing more concentrated than extraction.

Source: CEPII BACI (HS-2017), 3-year mean 2022–2024, exporter value shares → refining.json. Value, not tonnage: BACI quantities carry unit artifacts (they put one EU smelter at 66% of world zinc by weight but 13% by value). Honest scope: this is concentration of tradeable processed supply. A refiner that consumes its output at home — China turning imported concentrate into battery cathode, or into magnets — exports little refined metal and is understated here; that capacity shows up in the production / GeoPolRisk layer instead. This page is specifically about who controls the metal that crosses borders in processed form.

The refining you can’t see in trade — capacity vs exports, per metal

The last column above is the fix for this page’s central blind spot. Trade only sees refined metal that crosses a border; a country that refines and then uses the metal at home — China turning concentrate into battery cathode, magnets, chips — barely exports refined metal, so it vanishes from the wedge. Setting the reported refining-capacity share (IEA/USGS) beside the trade-export leader makes the gap explicit and per-metal:

Where the two columns agree (niobium→Brazil, nickel→Indonesia, tungsten & tantalum→China) the refined form is genuinely traded, so the wedge sees the chokepoint. Where they diverge — manganese, copper, bauxite, cobalt, lead, zinc — the metal is refined and consumed at home, so trade goes quiet and only capacity data reveals it. This is a curated, cited overlay, not a live feed: reported shares are approximate figures from IEA Global Critical Minerals Outlook 2025 and USGS MCS 2024/25. There is no clean machine-readable open dataset of refining capacity by country — the USGS production tables omit China at the refinery stage, and the facility-level capacity databases are proprietary — which is itself the honest reason this column is curated rather than computed.
